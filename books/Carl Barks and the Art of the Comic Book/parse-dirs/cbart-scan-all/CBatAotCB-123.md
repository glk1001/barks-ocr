HUEY, DEWEY AND LOUIE THE JUNIOR WOODCHUCKS Be Leery of Lake Eerie - 16 - script only - The Woodchucks try to go swimming in incredibly polluted Lake Eerie, but they find it infested with two kinds of strange beast: catfish that dine on beer cans and old tires, and a dragon with skin that looks like old plastic bags. (Jan. 27, 1972)

Changes: In the third panel on the last page of Barks's script, one of the nephews says of the dragon, "He seems to have passed away." In the story as published, this has been changed to "He seems to really be gone this time."

In the fourth panel, in the original script, one of the nephews asks, "What caused this disaster, doctors?" and one of two scientists replies, "The rains, without a doubt." In the published story, the scientist replies, "The rains! All that fresh water was too much for them."

In the fifth panel in Barks's script, one scientist says, "Fresh water diluted the lake until it was only 98 percent impure." The other scientist says, "The dragon couldn't take it. He was poisoned." In the published version, this panel has been replaced by one in which the delighted nephews exclaim that the lake is clean again.

In the final panel, in Barks's script, the lake is still polluted. The Woodchucks' leader, who is testing the water, says, "Scoriatic phosphates are bad today, troopers." One nephew, coating himself with material from a bucket, says, "Coat yourselves with graphite and wear gas masks." Another nephew — already coated and wearing a gas mask — is leaping into the lake and saying, "Last one in is a pink baboon." In the published version, the happy nephews are swimming in a clean Lake Eerie, and saying that they hope "someone can figure a way to keep it this way."

Barks: "Just noticed that there is another script I overlooked. 'Be Leery of Lake Eerie' 16 pages about pollution. It will be the next on the stands, I think. It concerns Lake Eerie which has become so full of acids, old tires, gunk and junk that the J.W.s have to wear grease when swimming. A giant dragon forms in the lake from the coalescence of chemical matters. He consumes junk in great mouthfuls. The J.W.s protect the dragon, but a big game hunter tries to kill it. Anyway, the office butchered my ending and it all comes out as sweet as raindrops on rose petals." (Aug. 16, 1972, letter to Michael Barrier)
