# BOYS' AND GIRLS'
# MARCH OF COMICS

This was a pulp-cover comic book given away to children by shoe stores, primarily. Its dimensions were the same as those of ordinary comic books, until 1952; later issues (none of which contained Barks's work) were half the size of ordinary comic books, and had slick covers.

## No number, but fourth in the series - 1947 - 32 pages

DONALD DUCK in "Maharajah Donald" - 28 - The nephews, starting with a pencil stub, trade up to a steam- ship ticket to India, which Donald commandeers from them. The nephews stow away, but are discovered. An Indian nobleman, the Maharajah of Hoopadoola, comes to their rescue, and to oblige him, Donald rides a sacred elephant to the neighboring kingdom of Bumpay. There he learns that he must increase his weight to a hundred pounds — or Bumpay will have to give fifty square miles of tiger-hunting land to the Maharajah of Hoopadoola, and Donald himself will be fed to the tigers. (Aug. 13, 1946)

DONALD DUCK in The Peaceful Hills - 2 - Donald forgoes seeing a cowboy movie with the nephews to take a walk in the snow-covered hills. (Oct. 4, 1946)

## 41 - 1949 - 24 pages

# CHEERIOS PREMIUMS

There were sixteen premium comic books in this series, each only a quarter the size of an ordinary comic book. The premium comics were the same width as full-size comics, but only

one-fourth as deep, so that each page contained a single row of panels.

## Y1 - 1947 - 32 pages

DONALD DUCK's Atom Bomb - 30 (equivalent to 7½ normal pages) - Donald invents an "atom bomb" that goes "fut" instead of "boom," and two professors are summoned to inspect Donald's handiwork. One of them, Professor Sleezy, a foreign expert, steals the bomb, and accidentally ignites it. (Sept. 9, 1946)

# CHRISTMAS IN DISNEYLAND (Dell)

## 1 - (December) 1957 - 100 pages

DONALD DUCK - 17 5/6 - Scrooge, Donald and the nephews risk an island's tabu for its black pearls, but almost immediately their ship is sucked from a lagoon into an underwater cavern. They plug a volcano to build up pressure to blow the boat out of the cavern. (Mar. 14, 1957)

These pages are three panels deep.

This story is actually a segment of the story "Christmas in Disneyland," which takes up most of the book; the Barks segment begins on the fourth page, not counting covers. The last panel on the last page of Barks's story is a transitional panel (not by Barks) leading from Barks's story to the next one.

Reprinted: Christmas Parade (Golden Press) No. 11191, 1977; Walt Disney Comics Digest No. 38, [December] 1972.
