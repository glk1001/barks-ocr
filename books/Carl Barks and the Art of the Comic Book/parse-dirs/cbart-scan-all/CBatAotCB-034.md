The "showings" ended when Eleanor Packer left Western in 1946. Barks's visits to Western's offices dwindled, and he worked thereafter mostly through the mail.

The stories that excited so much admiration in Western's offices in the mid-forties were, in fact, superior to most of what was being published in Western's comic books then, even though the general level of the artwork and writing in those comics was higher than it has ever been since. The comic books turned out by Western's New York office were, overall, the best; Walt Kelly, John Stanley, Morris Gollub, and Dan Noonan were doing remarkable work, under the benevolent supervision of Oskar Lebeck, for such titles as *Animal Comics* and *Little Lulu*. The Los Angeles office was responsible mainly for the comic books with animated characters (the Disney

comic books and *Looney Tunes*, for example). It had its share of good artists and writers—Buettner, Armstrong, Chase Craig, Tom McKimson—but Barks was clearly the class of the group.

Barks's years as a Disney story man showed strongly in his early stories. In discussing his years with Disney, Barks said, "I would say the key was, you have to have a reason for everything. If you could find a reason for something, you could drag anything in. I think that was what I got out of my Disney training more than anything, to analyze whether something was *necessary* to a story."

Barks's early stories in *Walt Disney's Comics* were solidly, logically constructed; in this respect, they were like miniature Disney cartoons. They were filled with "plot gags," one leading to another without any dawdling. The gags were sometimes brilliant, sometimes weak, but they were rarely careless or contrived. Still, there was something lacking. The ten-page stories in *Walt Disney's Comics* were not only like miniature cartoons, they were like skeletal cartoons. The characters' personalities were only sketched in; Donald and his three nephews, Huey, Dewey, and Louie, were stereotyped as adversaries, and little more.

Barks set the pattern in his first story. Donald derides the nephews for buying a lucky rabbit's foot, then changes his mind as they have good luck and he has bad. Finally, Donald "borrows" the rabbit's foot so that he can subdue an escaped gorilla and collect adulatory headlines. The rabbit's foot works only for its true owners, of course, and in due time Donald is in a hospital bed.

Barks's stories in the next four or five years were usually cast in this mold — conflict between Donald and the nephews. The nephews, rather passive at first, became steadily more belligerent over the years. They were Donald's competitors as kite builders, wire walkers, swimmers, skaters, and skiers, among other things, besides fighting with him over more normal parent-child problems.
