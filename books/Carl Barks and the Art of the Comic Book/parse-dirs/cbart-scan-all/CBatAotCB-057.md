The best stories with Gladstone were in fact stories about Donald, who was made to pay the price for taking Gladstone's luck as seriously as Gladstone did.

In one such story, in the May 1952 *Walt Disney's Comics*, Donald, the nephews, and Scrooge break into a safe that they think contains Gladstone's good luck charm. It contains only a dime, though, and after the frustrated ducks bully him a little, Gladstone explains: "Once, long ago, in a very weak moment, I took a job." "A job!" the shocked ducks cry in unison. "Yes!" Gladstone confesses as he breaks into tears. "I actually worked — I earned that dime. And I've been so ashamed of it ever since that I hid it in the safe and never looked at it again!" The ducks depart, silent and glassy-eyed.

Gyro Gearloose, the third of Barks's major supporting characters, did a walk-on in that same story, but it was in the next issue of *Walt Disney's Comics* that Gyro really made his debut. Gyro was not a duck, but a chicken — a chicken almost twice as tall as the ducks, with a mop of red hair and glasses perched on his beak. Clad, in his early appearances, in a baggy

shirt and pants, he was later given a vest, a haircut and an absurd little clown's hat held on with a string.

It took Scrooge and Gladstone almost two years to acquire their "specialities" — wealth and luck — but Gyro was, from the first, a fabulous inventor. In his first story, Gyro has invented "think boxes" that will teach any animals that pass through their rays how to think and speak. Donald scoffs at the "think boxes" — until a wolf speaking colloquial English has him tied to a spit, and is garnishing him with sage.

Gyro's history after that — first in occasional appearances in *Walt Disney's Comics*, and then, in 1956, in a regular story in *Uncle Scrooge* — was filled with equally weird and wonderful inventions. Trained worms that haul in fish while the fisherman waits, a machine that translates cat yowling into English and English into cat talk (with mutually unflattering results), a robot that picks up its master's thoughts and acts accordingly, a machine that lets anyone actually see what he imagines — even a machine that tracks fireflies, and a whole host of odd little inventions that pop up for a panel or two and are never seen again.
