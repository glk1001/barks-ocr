The idea for the cover — the nephews are putting hair curlers in a dog's hair — is the same as that of the cover for the December 1949 *Walt Disney's Comics*, which was drawn by Walt Kelly; the major difference is that Donald, rather than Daisy, is observing the nephews and the dog. October 27, 1950 - Model sheet of Donald and the nephews.

January 25, 1951 - Model sheet of Uncle Scrooge.

The purpose of these two model sheets is unclear; Barks has said of them, "I suppose the editors expected to hand them out to other duck strip artists." Barks wrote and illustrated virtually all of the Disney duck stories until 1950, when other artists began illustrating the stories in *Walt Disney's Comics* and back-up stories in *Donald Duck*. Some of these new artists' work was markedly inferior to Barks's work, and it may be that Western's editors hoped to use these model sheets to bring them into line.

Barks did a model sheet of Huey, Dewey and Louie as the Junior Woodchucks in 1971, but it was for the Disney studio's publications department, not for Western.

Model sheets or single drawings also exist for some other characters — Gyro Gearloose, Bombie the Zombie (from "Voodoo Hoodoo" in *Donald Duck* Four Color No. 238, 1949), and Mr. Jones — but these were for Barks's own reference; as he has explained, he would otherwise have had no drawings of such characters available from the time he submitted the first stories with them until those stories were published, since making copies of his artwork would have been difficult and expensive in those pre-Xerox days.

January 10, 1952 - "DONALD DUCK" for *Walt Disney's Comics* No. 144, September 1952.

This is listed in Barks's records as the "golden apples" story. He noted that it "must have been shelved." The story that was originally scheduled for *Walt Disney's Comics* No. 145 was substituted for it. The artwork for this story has vanished, but Barks remembers it as a modern version of the myth of Atlanta and the golden apples. He said this of it in the 1974 interview: "I only recall that I had Daisy quite angry with Donald because he was trying to win the hand, I guess, of this queen of the apple festival. . . . Every time that Donald would be about to catch up with this fleeing queen, or princess, or goddess, whatever she was, she would drop her golden apple and he would pick it up, and then he had to chase her again. . . . Daisy was so jealous that she was throwing things at Donald, and she was not acting in a ladylike manner. That was the objection. . . . That was the only excuse they ever gave me."

January 24, 1952 - "One Donald Duck gag for future use."

February 7, 1952 - One nephews gag, one Donald Duck gag, one Bugs Bunny gag "for future use."

In regard to the Bugs Bunny gag, Barks said, "I don't have any record of what the Bugs Bunny gag was about. The day being Feb. 7, 1952, I'd say the gag was probably sold to Western for use in the newspaper strip. This ten-page story is listed by Barks as "bobsled race," and he notes, "Don't know what became of this story. Probably scrapped." The artwork for this story has vanished, but it seems likely that it was shelved because it involved conflict between Donald and the nephews; it was less than six months later — around the time Western's editors would have been putting *Walt Disney's Comics* No. 196 together — that Barks was asked by an editor in Western's West Coast office to tone down the clashes among the ducks, because of parents' complaints.
