"I developed Uncle Scrooge for the same reason that I developed Gladstone Gander, the Beagle Boys, etc. It was just to broaden out the number of characters that I could use. Each one served a useful purpose in giving Donald something more to do and in taking the load off Donald and the nephews.

"I needed a situation in which Donald would have to go up in the mountains where there was snow and a mountain cabin and so on. I generally wrote stories by thinking first of a locale, and I had developed this mountain locale where Donald would have to find his own Christmas tree and maybe meet a bear. It just wasn't enough to build a story on, and I got to thinking that if I had a reason for

Donald to go there, I might find it easier to fill in all those empty pages. The introduction of Scrooge was in a Christmas story—I thought maybe if Donald had a rich uncle like Dickens' Scrooge, I might get some good out of that. The rich uncle would give Donald a reward if he went up there and showed himself to be a very brave duck.

"It wasn't until about the third story I wrote that I realized I had a character that I could use over and over again. Up to that time I had thought of him only as someone I could drag in once in a while for a little interest, but about the third story I did using Uncle Scrooge I realized that he had quite a lot of appeal."
