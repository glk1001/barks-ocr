An old gag man, Barks played the ducks for laughs; and from the start, a thinking, articulate Donald was funny. He frequently had Donald quietly assessing his impending annihilation as the world fell apart around him; the juxtaposition was far funnier than the quacking film duck. In WDCS No. 40, Donald sets out to show off his skiing expertise. When he is inadvertently shot off a snag-toothed crag, he muses as he hurtles toward the opposing mountain face, "Well, that gives me another two minutes to live!"

Donald's experiences are exaggerated, but they spring from recognizable human emotions. "I think one of the main appeals of these stories that I wrote was that they were so true to life. They were just about people and their crazy adventures—the dangers they went

through and the humorous things that they did. I never really went into any political meaning. I satirized a lot of things, and sometimes the satire might have been a little stinging to certain people. But really, I never thought of the political angle. In fact, I didn't believe that children, whom Western Publishing thought were buying all the comics, would be interested in political subjects."

While the content of a comic book may or may not have deep significance, comic book artists are in the business of turning out a product on a deadline. In Barks' case, this meant twelve monthly stories and up to five long books a year. Since Barks was both author and artist, one of his primary concerns was dreaming up enough situations for Donald and the nephews, and then getting those situations turned into stories and in to the editors on time.
