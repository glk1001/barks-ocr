# 24 CARL BARks: CONVersatIoNS

**BARKS:** Yes. I would once in a while try some girls or musclemen, just for a little fun.

**BRAY:** Would you prefer the human characters over, say, fantasy characters like the Gneezles or the Terries and Fermies?

**BARKS:** Oh, I guess so; it's hard to say. . . . I'd rather draw humans. Fantasy characters get into another field, but of course in comic books, you have to combine 'em all.

**BARRIER:** What kind of realistic stories would you have liked to have done?

**BARKS:** I was really hooked on the Alex Raymond sort of stuff—*Flash Gordon*. Another subject would have been western stories, although I was never good on drawing horses and probably never could [draw them well].

**BRAY:** As a kid, did you enjoy reading cartoons?

**BARKS:** Yeah, that's how I happened to take up cartooning. I was fascinated by cartoons.

**BRAY:** Who were some of your favorite cartoonists or strips?

**BARKS:** Well, I will say that *Happy Hooligan* wasn't among my favorites, but *Segar* and his early *Thimble Theatre*, and cartoonists you wouldn't recognize, who drew for the *San Francisco Examiner* [were favorites]. Back in those years, in the early 1920s and before that time, the *San Francisco Examiner* had a Sunday section of humor and cartoons, and, really, it was just like a big magazine folder. It was full of cartoonists' stuff; some of those guys were marvelous cartoonists. Those fellows were real draftsmen in pen and ink; they all had distinctive styles. I would read that *Examiner* Sunday section, and not copy their stuff, but try to draw a similar situation using their style.

**BRAY:** When you were drawing comics, did you ever go out and buy a comic book that wasn't put out by Western, or buy a newspaper for a strip you liked?

**BARKS:** Oh lord, yes. I've got a whole bible of Hal Fosters.

**BRAY:** It kind of shows in your outdoor splash panels.

**BARKS:** Hal Foster was a tremendous influence.

**BARRIER:** Were you conscious of influences from outside the comics—films you'd seen that made an impression on you, boys' adventure books, things like that?

---



**BARKS:** Oh, sure. All those things, they were all stored up there in my head, like getting out old reels of microfilm and running them through. In fact, I think that's all memory is; a person's own character is just composed of rolls of microfilm that are run through again and again. And I think that's how ideas come about. You run through two or three reels of that old microfilm that's up in the head, and the combination comes out like a new idea.
