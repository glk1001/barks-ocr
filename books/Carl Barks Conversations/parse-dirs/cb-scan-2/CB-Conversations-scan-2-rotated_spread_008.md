PC: Your stories were always accepted. You almost never had stories rejected, right?

CB: Very seldom ever had anything rejected—it would be just parts of a story, a couple of times, that I had to revise a little bit. Whenever they rejected a story, it was rejecting the whole thing, and it was only about three stories in all those years.

PC: There was the one about "Silent Night," where Donald was Christmas caroling.

CB: Yeah, well, that was when I was just starting up in the business. Surprisingly they paid me for it and then rejected it.

PC: I wonder what the reason was?

CB: Oh, the reason was that I was having him sing "Silent Night," a sort of sacred song. It apparently looked to the editors as if I was making fun of the song "Silent Night."

PC: Of course, you were making a joke about Donald Duck being so noisy?

CB: Yeah, that's what I was really making fun of—it wasn't what he was singing. *He* was committing the sacrilege. It wasn't me, it was Donald, the way he was singing, how he forced people to listen. And they did kind of have an idea that it might offend people who go around singing Christmas carols. I think the people who go around singing Christmas carols have so damn much fun out of life, they'd laugh like hell over gags about themselves.

PC: That's exactly how I felt. I wonder why the editors at Western Publishing felt that way.

CB: They had a different sense of humor, a very cautious sense of humor, I would say. If anything looked like it might offend somebody, well, they were scared to death of it. It just no longer looked funny to them.

PC: I have a feeling, myself, after talking and reading the comics, that maybe the publishers thought that their comics really weren't that good, and therefore, it was more important to try not to offend someone than to try to make a story as good and as exciting and as interesting as can be.

CB: That's right, play it safe. They didn't take any gambling chances on producing any work of genius. Play it safe.

PC: They didn't think their books were having any influence on kids?
