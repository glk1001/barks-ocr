She came out and they were married, and a year or two later my brother was born, and then I was born, and that was the way life went.

**BARRIER:** Had your parents met in Missouri, then?

**BARKS:** Oh, yes, they had gone to school together as kids back in Missouri. They hadn't seen each other over all those years; he remembered her as a little towheaded girl.

**BARRIER:** So they had corresponded for more than twenty years?
**BARKS:** I guess so.

**BARRIER:** That's a wonderful love story.

**BARKS:** It must have been. Well, that's the way things were in those days. When a guy set out for the pioneering life in the West, he just cut all ties, except by the Pony Express or something, although the railroad, I guess, had been built by the time my dad got into California. I remember him telling about riding on top of the boxcars coming out to California. There was some guy sitting up ahead of him who was smoking a pipe, and the wind blew the sparks out of the pipe and set the guy's clothes afire. They had to roll him around and had a devil of a time putting that fire out.

**BARRIER:** His life must have rubbed off on you to some extent, giving you ideas for stories.

**BARKS:** No, I don't think so. My dad was never much of a fellow to talk; he was a silent sort of guy. He would read his Bible . . . that was about the extent of his life.

**BARRIER:** Your parents sound like strong, individual people.

**BARKS:** Oh, they were the salt of the earth . . . tillers of the soil.
