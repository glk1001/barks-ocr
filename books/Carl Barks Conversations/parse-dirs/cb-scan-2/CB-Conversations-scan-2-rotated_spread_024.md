**BARKS:** Well, I would say that the old Chaplin movies and the old Harold Lloyd movies and—way before your time—the old Douglas Fairbanks movies . . . now, those kind of things—not that I considered them good, but I considered them very instructive, especially the Chaplin movies, the old Harold Lloyd movies, and so on. You could learn so much from just looking at them. You see one of them through once, and there were so many memorable things. The way those things were timed—it just stuck with me. I think that was where I got my impressions.

**BARRIER:** When you were writing and drawing your own stories, were you conscious of any influences other than your Disney training?

**BARKS:** There were a lot of influences. Disney training just helped me to organize all those influences.

**BARRIER:** What would you describe as some of your influences?
**BARKS:** For instance, my sense of dramatics, and the sense of pathos, and the sense of just slapstick humor. Each of those is a field that is practically isolated by itself, yet with my Disney training, I was able to put all three of those elements into one story, and they'd all fit together naturally.

**BARRIER:** What was there about the Disney training that let you do this?
**BARKS:** I would say the key was you have to have a reason for everything. And if you could find a reason for something, you could drag anything in. I think that what I got out of my Disney training more than anything was to analyze whether anything was necessary to a story. And so, when I felt I needed a little drama in here right now, tears rather than a laugh, why I could bring it in. My training at Disney's had showed me how to bring that in. You have a wipe-over and bring it in with a smash, that's the way to do it. Hit 'em right in the jaw. But it had to be related to what had happened before, or very quickly tied back to what had happened before.

**BARRIER:** What differences did you notice when you switched from cartoons to comic books? Did you find you could not do certain things in comics that you could do in animation? Were there certain limits on what you could do in each medium?

**BARRIER:** Getting back to your travel for a minute—you've done so many stories set overseas, but you've never been overseas, have you?
**BARKS:** Never been.

**BARRIER:** Whenever you were doing stories about the ducks in foreign countries, did you ever feel handicapped by not having been there?
**BARKS:** I think it was the other way around. It was an advantage never having been there. I created a sort of fantasy atmosphere about them that probably would have been a little bit stiff if I had tried to draw from real life. That's my travels there, those *National Geographics*.

**BARRIER:** I was thinking this morning that you never sent the ducks to any real American cities; you never saw the ducks, as I can recall, in a real New York City or a real San Francisco. Of course, you had them in Los Angeles, but it wasn't usually called Los Angeles.

**BARKS:** Specific locales within the United States I felt were not good, because these things were translated into a lot of foreign languages. What seemed like a big difference to us in America—say, between Los Angeles and Kansas City—over in Germany, they're just alike. All these different countries wouldn't appreciate that difference. I always had in the back of my mind the translation difficulties, foreign readers understanding what my subject matter was, and what was behind the subject matter.
