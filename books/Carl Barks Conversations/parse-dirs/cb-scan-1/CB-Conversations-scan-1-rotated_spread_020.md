**1975**: Edward Summer conducts the first film interviews with Barks in Goleta (Apr.), funded by a grant from the National Endowment for the Arts; E. B. Boatner's essay "Comics 101b (half term): Why a Duck?" appears in the May issue (Vol. 77, No. 9) of *The Harvard Magazine*; Ault and Thomas Andrae conduct the first videotape interview with Barks in Goleta (Aug.); Dorfman and Mattelart's *How to Read Donald Duck: Imperialist Ideology in the Disney Comic* (from the 1971 Chilean original *Para Leer al Pato Donald*) is published in English, translated with an introduction by David Kunzle; in *Jag, Farbror Joakim* (*I, Uncle Scrooge*) Sture Hegerfors writes at length about Barks and his work in his foreword; *Les aventures explosives de Donald* (translated from Italian) is published, marking the first mention of Carl Barks's name in France.

**1976**: Paints *Porky of the Mounties* for the cover of the *Overstreet Price Guide*; Kivekas publishes the first index of Barks's comics in Finland; Barks's oil painting *July 4th in Duckburg* sells for a record $6,400 at auction in New York City; Disney withdraws permission for Barks to produce any more Disney oil paintings; Barks paints *King Midas Having Fun*, the first of a new series of non-Disney oils; on 22-24 Oct., makes his first major public appearance at the Newcon convention in Boston, Massachusetts; the Donaldist Society in Sweden (Nationella Ankistförbundet i Sverige [kvack]) is founded; Hans von Storch publishes the first issue of *Der Hamburger Donaldist* magazine.

**1977**: The Donaldist Society in Germany (Deutsche Organisation Nichtkommerzieller Anhänger des Lauteren Donaldismus [D.O.N.A.L.D.]) is founded. Carl and Garé move to Temecula, California; attends the San Diego Comic Convention (Aug.); "The Man Who Drew Ducks" by Paul Ciotti appears in *[PSA] California* magazine (Nov.).

**1977-82**: Following the withdrawal of the Disney painting license, prices of Barks's Disney oil paintings sharply increase to five figures, by the early 1980s approaching $50,000; Barks produces a series of non-Disney oil paintings entitled "Kings and Queens of Myths and Legend," two of which are reproduced as lithographs; produces a series of (mostly ink and watercolor) paintings based on his concept of "famous figures of history as they might have looked if their genes had gotten mixed up with waterfowl"; completes two oil paintings based on a western theme, *Well-Armed Stranger* and *Worth a Whole Month's Wages* (both 1982).

**1978-80**: Summer and Gary Kurtz (producer of *Star Wars*) reach an agreement with Disney to publish a limited edition hardbound collection of Uncle Scrooge stories and gain permission for Barks to produce a new Disney oil painting for lithographic reproduction in conjunction with the Uncle Scrooge book; Hamilton, Cochran, et al. reach an agreement with Disney to publish a hardbound book containing reproductions of Barks's Disney oil paintings.

**1981**: Barrier's *Carl Barks and the Art of the Comic Book*, the first thoroughly researched biographical, critical, and bibliographic account of Barks and his work, is published; Hamilton and Cochran's *The Fine Art of Walt Disney's Donald Duck by Carl Barks* (Another Rainbow Publishing) is published as a signed, limited edition; *Uncle Scrooge McDuck: His Life and Times*, edited by Summer and published by Kurtz, contains a signed, limited edition lithograph of *Wanderers of Wonderland*, Barks's first Disney oil since 1976, as well as "Go Slowly, Sands of Time," a new Uncle Scrooge story by Barks, illustrated in ink and watercolor.
