**Q:** What made you decide on a career in art?

**A:** I was looking for an easier way to make a living.

**Q:** What had you been doing?

**A:** I was working in a railroad car shop, on the riveting gang. That was in 1929, and the Depression had just started. I quit the railroad yard and there was no other work available. I freelanced for comics, in drawing and joke-writing. Mostly joke-writing, because I couldn't draw very well at the time.

**Q:** It was probably very difficult in those Depression years to be freelancing.

**A:** It was.

**Q:** And then, in late 1935, you got a job with the Disney Studios?
**A:** Yes, that's right. I tried out for one month and then got hired at $20 a week.

**Q:** You had no formal art training—you just taught yourself in the intervening years?

**A:** Yes, and I was a mighty poor instructor, too.

**Q:** What sort of work did you do for Disney?

**A:** I started as an in-betweener [drawing frames between action in animated cartoons]. After about six months, I wasn't working out very well as an in-betweener, but in the meantime, I had managed to sell a few gags to the story department and the comic strip department, and they figured I might be all right as a story man, so they put me in the story department.

**Q:** Which means you did no art for awhile?

**A:** No drawing, except for the story board, which is just a very rough form and is merely to show the continuity of the story.

**Q:** Would you say that working in the Disney story department helped you to write the stories for your own *Donald Duck* and *Uncle Scrooge* comics?

**A:** You bet. I learned how to write stories. You had to, or you were cut.

**Q:** Do you recall offhand any particular cartoons you worked on—did you work on *Snow White* or *Pinocchio*?

**A:** No, I didn't do anything on *Snow White* or *Pinocchio*. I was just a duck man—strictly a duck man.

**Q:** Not even any *Silly Symphonies*? Just the Donald Duck cartoons?
**A:** Just *Donald Duck*. Oh, I helped out on a *Pluto* once in a while as things moved from story unit to story unit—in story conferences we'd submit gags to another guy's story.

**Q:** You never wrote the storyline for a complete cartoon?

**A:** There was no such thing as an individual doing that. It was a group effort.

**Q:** Would you say, in the main, that you enjoyed working at the Disney Studios? Did Disney Productions later control your comic-book work at all?

**A:** No. I didn't like the pressure and the fact there were so many straw-bosses looking over your shoulder to see how you were doing, criticizing your work all the time. I can't take criticism. I didn't enjoy working for Disney in the studio. He expected miracles from his gag men and story crews. On comics, Disney Productions, I was told, looked at all finished stories before they were sent East.

**Q:** You said that you started working for Whitman in 1942. Did this have anything to do with the fact that Disney was hurt a good bit by the outbreak of the war—that they had to stop doing a good bit of their cartoon work?

**A:** Well, yes, we were commencing to do a bunch of war propaganda pictures and educational stuff for the Army and medical propaganda and all that, that I didn't like and it seemed so much better to get out of that studio while I could get out before I got locked in—because once they hung a badge on your desk and told you, "This is your job," you were frozen there. So I got out while I still could.

**Q:** You left the Disney Studio in November 1942, after you did your first *Donald Duck* comic, "Pirate's Gold"?

**A:** Yes, that's right. I was going to raise chickens and do anything that I could down here in San Jacinto, where the air was kind to my sinuses. So I came down here and right away I found out that Whitman was pretty anxious to have a ten-page *Donald Duck* story, so I submitted some. I had done a little bit for them in that "Pirate's Gold" story in the summer of 1942, while I was still working at the studio. I happened to be in the Duck unit and they needed somebody who was used to drawing the ducks and the animators were too busy, so it just fell on a story man.
