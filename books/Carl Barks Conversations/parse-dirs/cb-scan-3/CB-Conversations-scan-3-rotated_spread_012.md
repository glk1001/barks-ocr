**Q: Did you have any problems with censorship?**

**BARKS:** It's pretty well known that I had censorship on that Klondike story and the "Trick or Treat" story and the milkman story, and the story of Donald's footrace with Daisy and some girl—the story of the golden apples. There was not much in the way of small censorship, but then I was so careful with my own censorship that I didn't run into that problem much. It was just on the big things like the Klondike story that I got slapped with much censorship. I knew that I couldn't put in much in the way of violence: it had to be comical violence that didn't seem to hurt anybody. I knew that I was not to glorify crime. I could have the Beagle Boys always stealing, but I tried to do it in a very comical way, so that it actually was not a crime that a child could imitate. It had to be a crime that was just in a world of fantasy: kids could read about it but not in any way imitate it.

**Q: What about the problem of death? Was it ever difficult to relate humor to the fact that these characters could die?**

**BARKS:** The characters had to be in danger of death in order to create suspense in a story; they had to be in real danger. And when you figure that the very ultimate in danger is the fear of death itself, you just about have to use it. But I always dramatized this problem in such a comical way that you didn't think back on it and get morbid about the fact that they had been exposed to deadly dangers.
