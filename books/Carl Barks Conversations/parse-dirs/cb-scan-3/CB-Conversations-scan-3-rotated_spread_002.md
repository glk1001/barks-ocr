Well, before I had finished the period of training, I was put over into the story department. In fact, I don't think I would ever have finished the period of training before I got fired, but I turned in so many gags to the comic strip department and also to the story department whenever they would ask for some gag assistance from the hoi polloi around the place, to pick up a little extra money, that I got put in the story department on a permanent basis.

**Q: Was drawing comics a lot more personally satisfying than working in animation?**

**CB:** Oh, Lord, yes. I couldn't have ever been an animator. It would have been too monotonous for me. The comic books were a tremendous field for presenting ideas and all kinds of activity and background. There's a wide range to the artwork that goes into comic books. In animation you just draw on a little bit of a character repeatedly over and over for days at a time. You get no background. Just a lighted glass under your characters. Oh, that'd be terrible!

**Q: How did you get into doing comics?**

**CB:** I have no inside dope on how Western decided to start using original ten-page Donald Duck stories in *Walt Disney's Comics*. Obviously they were running out of newspaper strip reprints and needed some original stuff to fill the book. I didn't know they planned the strip at the time I left Disney's. They sent me a ready-written script to draw for story #1, the crow story, several weeks after I left the studio. Maybe the fact that I was available caused them to send the work to me. Otherwise it would have gone to some other artist. I noticed some bad errors in the plot and wrote the editor for permission to change things around. The result was that they invited me to see if I could do a whole original story myself. The story of the kids and their "rabbit foot" was the result. I used an animated cartoon approach to story plotting in my early comic books because that approach had become second nature after seven years in the studio story department.

I ordinarily worked on only one story at a time. I never could comfortably mix them up or start a new one while one was still unfinished. The stories were a continuous chain-of-thought thing with me. They grew and were subject to change right up to the inking of the last panel. Besides, I liked a little mental rest after finishing a story.

**Q: Could you talk a bit about how your comics version of Donald differed from the screen version?**

**CB:** While working in the story department on the duck I had become quite familiar with his character as we had it in the animated cartoons. When I got to "own" the story, I was pretty much on my own. Oh, I broadened his character out very much. Instead of making just a quarrelsome little guy out of him, I made a sympathetic character. He was sometimes a villain, and he was often a real good guy and at all times he was just a blundering person like the average human being, and I think that is one of the reasons people like the duck.

**Q: What about the nephews—how they related to Donald and how they treated him . . .**

**CB:** The nephews are also a holdover from the animation days and in the studio we used to have them just mischievous little guys that were always in conflict with Donald. Now when I used them in the comic books, I found that was too limited a field, so I began making them into sort of smart little guys once in a while, and very clumsy little guys at other times, and always, I aimed at surprise in each story so that nobody could pick up a comic book and say, "Well, the nephews are going to behave thus and so." They wouldn't know until they had read the story just what those little guys were going to be up to in a particular sequence. So I broadened them like I did Donald, started out with some mischievous little guys and ended up with little scientists, you might say. They were hard to draw, too, believe me.

**Q: What are the differences between your ducks and human beings?**

**CB:** I guess I was confused on that. I'm afraid that there were times when I kept thinking of those ducks as human beings and treating them as human beings, and I even reached a point in the story, the first issue of *Uncle Scrooge*, I called him "Only a Poor Old Man." I didn't call him a poor old duck. So, I guess I always felt they were human beings, just humans with ducks' bodies. All their problems were the same as humans had. I guess that I humanized them.
