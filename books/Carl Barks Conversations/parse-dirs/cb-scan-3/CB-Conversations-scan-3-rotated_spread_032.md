**H:** Erika Fuchs did translate the whole spirit, the meaning of the words.

**B:** I don't know how she did it—she must have had a very good knowledge of English.

**H:** What was the first comic strip you ever saw in your life?
**B:** Oh, that was a long time ago; it was in my childhood around 1906 or 1907.

**H:** What was it? *Little Nemo*?

**B:** Yes, *Little Nemo* was the first comic that I remember—and then came *Happy Hooligan* and others, but I most clearly remember Winsor McCay's *Little Nemo*—wonderful drawings.

**H:** Did you imagine back then that you would be drawing comics yourself?

**B:** I believe so. I always wanted to know how to make something like this and I had a great desire to try it myself.

**H:** These stories used to appear in the Sunday paper in those days, didn't they?

**B:** In the Sunday papers, yes. We got the paper around Tuesday. In San Francisco the paper appeared on Saturday or Sunday, but we would get it up there at our home ranch at about Tuesday of the following week. We were living on this ranch up in eastern Oregon.

**H:** Which comic strip inspired you the most?

**B:** Oh, I've been asked that question many times. I can't think about any less than a dozen of them.

**H:** I guess *Popeye the Sailor* was one of them?

**B:** One of them could have been *Popeye the Sailor*. I loved the stories, but I didn't care much for the drawings.

**H:** You liked the stories?

**B:** Oh, the stories were very funny, yes.

**H:** And strange.

**B:** Yes, the construction of the stories, and the way the gags were phrased, and the way these absurd characters would be introduced. Weird—and they made no sense. Fascinating (laughs).

[Interruption of tape]

**H:** What was the first story you wrote yourself?

**B:** That one here—that was in spring of 1943. They sent me a script from the studio—or from the editorial office. So then I got to do the second and third story, and as I went along, I was beginning to get pretty well established writing stories. At that time it was OK to make black ducks; in those days it was still possible to draw these black ducks. But today nobody would dare to do that, because it is insulting the Negroes.

**H:** Well, I like those black ducks.

**B:** But look at this duck with the earrings; that would be totally impossible today.

**H:** Apart from Donald was there any other Disney character you liked?

**B:** There was one I couldn't stand; it was Disney's Goofy.

**H:** Why?

**B:** Goofy was simply a half-wit. I could never understand what was supposed to be funny about a half-wit. I liked Mickey for his purposes. He was good in adventure strips. But the thought of having to draw something like this did not appeal to me. I enjoyed working with the duck—because I could knock him around and have him get hurt. I could let him fall off cliffs and everything, you know. It was lots of fun with Donald. With Mickey it would have been kind of dangerous, because Mickey always had to be right. He always had to come out the winner. With the duck I had a comedian that I could treat badly and who I could make fun of.

**H:** Donald was the loser type.

**B:** That's right, he was a kind of loser.
