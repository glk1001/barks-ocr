I never got a swelled head over hearing that *WDC&S* was selling millions of copies; I knew it wouldn't last. And besides, Alice Cobb, one of the editors, told me *my work* wasn't selling those comics—it was Walt Disney's name over the titles.

Now that I am old and cranky, and Western and *WDC&S* are dust in the pages of history, I have other things to write about.

The "dangerous" pill, Metabolife, seems to be improving my day-to-day feeling of being alive. I swallow one pill an hour before breakfast and one-half a pill an hour before lunch. The effect is sort of an energy surge that keeps me interested in whatever is interesting. I can think, and usually I need only a short nap in the afternoon to keep my inner machinery going.

In prior months I was tired *all the time* and felt thick-headed. Also I weighed 185. Now I weigh 170.

I watched some of the TV program about increasing people's ages. Am glad I won't have to suffer such a fate.

Best of luck to you and your family.

Carl

## Questions and Answers

**Question 1:**

According to Mike Barrier's account of your payment records, you are credited with "art only" on all of the *WDC&S* front cover gags that are listed from issues September '49 to April '54 (including some famous ones such as the sailboat and the eggbeater outboard motor). If this is true, how were cover gags sent to you—as sketches, as verbal descriptions, or what? How much restaging of such prepackaged gags did you do or were you expected to do? Would you have drawn up small versions of gags you did not invent yourself? Since I vividly recall your mentioning to me that the eggbeater gag was your own, I would frankly be surprised if you really did "art only" on many of those covers, even though you may not have been paid for the ideas or have a record of the payment. Do you recall if any of the *WDC&S* cover gags in this period might have been your own inventions?

**In reply to Question 1:**

**In reply to Question 2:**

I think I lived in the remodeled warehouse through most of 1951 and 1952. The warehouse had been a barn for carnival rides and equipment. It had a corrugated iron roof that was noisy in hailstorms. The owner, who had sold out his carnival, put together some partitions and kitchens and bathrooms and made the place into five small "apartments." My second wife had divorced me, and I moved into one of the apartments with my only remaining possessions: a drawing board, clothes, and two blankets.
