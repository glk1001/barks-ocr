**DA:** You were that broke, huh?

**CB:** Oh yeah. Hell, yes. I had barely enough money for rent or an apartment for a month, and before I got my first paycheck, I was just reduced to eating fig newtons. That was my breakfast, lunch, and dinner. It was kind of risky business, but I managed to get on the job there at Disney. Since then I have been a Disney guy.

**DA:** I guess you could say that.

**CB:** Even though I quit Disney's in 1942, I have been associated with them ever since in various kinds of work.

**DA:** One of the things that is said is that you were exploited by Disney, but you feel that actually

**CB:** I exploited them a little bit, yeah—in turn, sure. God, yes. Just think of all the good things Disney did for me. And so I feel that anytime I can do something in the way of drawing, signing of lithographs or sketches, anything that will help Disney make some money, why I'm glad to do it. Trying to pay them back for the favors they did me.

**DA:** You retired 31 years ago, and you said to me a few days ago on the phone, "Oh, I wish I could have had a normal retirement." I am just curious. I will put it a couple of different ways. What kind of example do you think you have set for people with your life as opposed to the stories? That is, the fact that you kept working and producing stuff and coming up with new stuff year after year when most people are just lounging around or playing shuffle board. Do you think it is important that you have done this?

**CB:** Oh, I guess it is. It shows that if a guy works hard enough at something, it is almost the same as a guy who retires and picks up a hobby and he works hard at that hobby. I was entertaining myself as I went along and getting paid for it.

**DA:** What about the hassle part of it?

**CB:** Well, the hassles came in, it seems like, after I had all that career. It

If sometime in the future somebody did something that made it look like a bad idea to paint, why they would always stop me. And that is what happened. And I think it was awfully good of Disney, because not only was George Sherman involved in that, I think there were some other higher ups in there like Roy Disney himself.

**DA:** Oh really? Well he introduced you when you got that Disney Legends Award.

**CB:** That's right. And he spoke to me as if he was a fan of mine. Like he had read those comic book stories when he was a kid growing up. And he liked my stuff that I did for Disney, so we had a sort of mutual understanding—that I liked what Disney did for me, and he liked what I did for Disney.
