They've all of a sudden discovered that I was around [laughs]. I could still do a little work, so they wanted to get me to paint these duck paintings. **CB:** Three months just to paint this [Points at the money bin with all its coins]. This was a big painting. It was . . . let's see, twenty-eight inches high and thirty-five inches wide. Big fellow. [The painting was sold for two hundred thousand dollars.]

**ES:** Now you retired from comic book drawing in 1966. I know you've done some scripts since then, but are you planning anything else?

**CB:** No. [chuckling] I'm not planning to do any more. I did write one story around the end of the year. I wrote a twenty-four-page Uncle Scrooge adventure. Like I was saying, whenever you want to write an easy story, you just get ol' Uncle Scrooge. So I sent him off on a treasure hunt, and he finds the Trojan horse. It'll be drawn by William Van Horn—guy up in Vancouver, British Columbia. [A promising young Disney artist, Van Horn was chosen by Barks himself, I was later told. # ERik Svane / 1994 169

**ES:** How many fan letters do you get every day?

**CB:** Oh, not so many. Because for many years, I just stopped answering fan letters, and gradually, they just dropped off. Most of the fan letters were people wanting an autograph. So when they didn't get any autograph, they got mad at me and they didn't write any more. Oh, but I get, oh, maybe as many as ten a month, and each one requires an answer, and it takes me almost all day to compose an answer, because those letters are almost like a compact interview. They want to know so many things, and they want to tell me so many things that I—it just takes an awful lot of time to answer. So I made up a form letter to send out and tell them that just because of time and so on, I couldn't answer but I thank them very profusely for being so interested in the ducks.

**ES:** Now I'd like to go back to your beginnings. Do you still subscribe to the *National Geographic*?

**CB:** Oh yes. Whenever they send me the bill now, they say "valued subscriber since 1935."

**ES:** What is your earliest memory?

**CB:** Oh . . . I think my earliest memory that comes quickly to mind was going to school in southern Oregon.

**ES:** Good memory?

**CB:** Yeah, it was a good memory. I lived almost a mile from the schoolhouse, little ol' one-room schoolhouse in the sagebrush. My brother, who was a little older than I, would walk to school [with me] and the meadow larks were singing and the birds were all happy and everything looked so nice and sweet.

**ES:** Do you have any regrets about not continuing in one job?

**CB:** Oh, I was so glad to leave the farm and that hard work.
