**LA:** And did she go with him, so you didn't see much of her?

**CB:** Oh, yeah, she had a ton of countries to live in, and they never had any children. They were just frantic about it. Finally, she got pregnant. That daughter is one that doesn't want to have any children at all. That line in the family just died right there. Yeah, my daughter, her husband finally died of a heart attack.

**DA:** And she's still living?

**CB:** She's living in Bremerton, Washington.

**DA:** And what's her first name?

**CB:** Her name is, uh, Dorothy. It was Peggy was the oldest one, and Dorothy, the younger one.

**LA:** Did Peggy have any children?

**CB:** She had three.

**DA:** And those are your grandchildren?

**CB:** That's where I get all these grandchildren and great-great grandchildren.

**DA:** Do you ever see them? Do you ever visit with them?

**CB:** Oh, once in a while. Dr. [Gerry] Tank took me up to see them a couple of years ago.

**DA:** I mean, do they know who you are and what you've done and all that kind of stuff?

**CB:** Oh, they have a kind of understanding, but none of them are comic book readers. Peggy was, the one who died. She took an interest in the comic books. She seemed to sense what it was that made the things funny and what it was that made a story hang together.

**DA:** Did she ever help you with your stories?

**CB:** Well, she sold me little ideas for stories every once in a while. I would pay her $10 a piece for any plot she could send me, even if it only took about 10 words to mention what the plot was about.

**LA:** What years were these?

**CB:** Well, they were 1950s, not so much in the 60s. She had her first cancer problem in about 1960.

# DONALD AULT AND LYNDA AULT / 1997

**LA:** Did some of her stories end up in your 10-pagers?

**CB:** Oh, yeah. A number of them I took and developed into stories.

**DA:** Were there any of the big stories that she was the source of?

**CB:** No, I don't think there was.

**DA:** And when Garé came along and started working with you did, was she helpful, too, I mean as a critic of your stories?

**CB:** Oh, yes. She was interested in them from the ground up, but the early years before Garé came along, I didn't have anybody that I could call in to look at the story with me—unlike the years down at the Disney studio. Whenever a story crew got a bunch of stuff up on the board, then they'd bring in a whole bunch of other story men to look at it. I would just be my own critic and just look at the stuff that was up there and analyze it and so on, and as time went by, and Garé came onto the scene, why, of course, then I did have somebody to look at the stories.

## Early Education

**LA:** Was there something in your childhood in those early years that you read that you still think of now?

**CB:** I had little access to books.

**LA:** That's too bad.

**CB:** In the little library we had in the one-room schoolhouse we had some of the King Arthur stories. We had books of poetry like Longfellow, those kinds of books. They were the classics. They had some of the Charles Dickens stories there.

**DA:** Something just occurred to me. I take it you actually did read Dickens's *Christmas Carol* with Ebenezer Scrooge?

**CB:** I did. I did years and years ago. I read *A Christmas Carol*. It seemed to me like it was a little hard to believe. He had these spirits of Christmas present and spirits of Christmas past that could be conjured up and have flesh and bones. It just seemed like a little too much magic mixed in with everything. It wasn't convincing. I just couldn't see how it would affect Uncle Scrooge to that point.

**DA:** You said in the last video interview that we did with you back in 1975 that that is the classic of all Christmas stories.

**CB:** It was for everybody else. I go along with whatever their opinion was.
