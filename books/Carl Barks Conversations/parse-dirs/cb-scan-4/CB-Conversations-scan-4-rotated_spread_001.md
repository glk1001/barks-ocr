**MN**: Did you do this when you were in San Francisco?

**CB**: Yes. I was working for a little printing plant, and they had a job of doing this poster that was going to be put in all the streetcars and so on, and they asked me to do it.

**MN**: Were you paid for that job?

**CB**: No, I didn't get anything extra. I think I was getting $12.50 a week.

**MN**: And that took care of your room, your board?

**CB**: Everything.

**MN**: What were your responsibilities once you arrived at the [Disney Studio] story department?

**CB**: Well, the responsibility was to think of stuff that was funny and think of it fast (laughs).

**MN**: What kind of boss was Walt Disney?

**CB**: Oh, he was an excellent boss—he was more of a genius, of course, in other things than he was in relations with his employees. We all were scared of him, but I don't think he was as vicious as we thought he was.

**MN**: Did he drop in unannounced?

**CB**: No. He found out if he dropped in unannounced, he had everybody scared all the time, so he let you know in advance that he was going to be there at a certain hour.

**MN**: Was there a secret network of information that let you know when the boss was coming down the hall?

**CB**: No, we didn't resort to that.

**MN**: What were your proudest accomplishments while you were at the studio?

**CB**: I guess, the first one was the fact that I did that barber chair gag and got into the story department. And then Harry Reeves and I put out a seven-minute short called "Good Scouts" with Donald and the kids going to Yellowstone National Park, and there we had a chance to put in a whole bunch of good gags, with Donald setting up his tent over a geyser, and brought in a bear and all. Walt gave Harry and I each a $300 bonus.

**CB**: You learned there that you don't waste any footage, don't waste any pages in comics—make the darned story tight and full of value from one panel to the next.

**MN**: Is there a difference between writing for animation and writing for comic books?

**CB**: Very much so.

**MN**: What are the main differences in your mind?

**CB**: Well, with comic books you don't stick with one particular set of actions. You try to move it along; it is a plot business. Animation is an action business; with action gags you think of something a character can do that is a funny routine, and then you try to get as much footage out of that routine as you possibly can.

**MN**: More concentration in plot development and storyline in a comic book?

**CB**: Oh, my yes. What else is there? It's a story you're trying to tell, and you try to tell it in the funniest way possible. So it's a combination of drawing and of trying to get something real funny, a good laugh.

**MN**: What is the difference between your Donald in the comic books and the Donald of animated cartoons?

**CB**: The animated Donald back in those days was a squatty guy who seemed to have rather rubbery legs, and I couldn't seem to get the kind of action I needed. I needed a Donald who could walk like a human. So I started making his legs straighter and his arm action and body a little bit taller than it was in the animated stuff.

**MN**: Did this lead to a different personality for Donald?

**CB**: The personality change came through the gags that I had to do for him. I found that I was very limited if I stayed with just the one Donald who was a squawky, fighty guy. I had to have a Donald who could do some thinking.
