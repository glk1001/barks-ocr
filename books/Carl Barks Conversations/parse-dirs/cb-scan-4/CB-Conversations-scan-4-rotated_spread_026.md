**DA:** You think that the kinds of things that they're being entertained by could actually prevent them from understanding comics or at least being interested in them?

**CB:** Well, they would not be interested in that; that is something that is like the old woodcuts. Something obsolete.

**DA:** If the comic book and comic strip narrative art form dies it'll be a tremendous loss to the culture.

**CB:** It's not going to die, exactly; it's just going to be replaced by a different way of putting it before people's eyes. Like the internet—where you read stuff on a monitor instead of in a book. It isn't like going to bed with a comic book where you can read it, turn the pages, slam the thing down, and pick it up later. It's giving away a tremendous amount of the convenience of reading in order to just put everything into some digital form so that some makers like Sony can make a lot more money selling stuff. . . . I don't hardly think that a way will come in which they can draw those comic book panels on the monitor screen with a mouse. It may be that they can. It may be that that's the form it will take—just make up these whole comic book pages right on the screen and draw in all the things with the mouse. It wouldn't have the spontaneity, the look of reality that you get from drawing these out with the blue pencil. . . . You get a thoroughness in the expression and all which I don't think you could ever get with a mouse and a line on the computer. I remember what Marc Davis, who was one of Disney's "Nine Old Men," had to say about *The Lion King*, that they looked like stuffed lions. Yeah, his opinion of animation done with the mouse was that it never quite came up with a real expression that looked genuine, or an action that looked genuine.

**DA:** Do you think that it's inherent in the medium, or maybe the artists haven't arrived yet who can make use of that medium.

**CB:** I don't think they can get it in that medium.

**DA:** Do you think the actual physical contact with the page has something to do with it, that it's touchable, tangible? Because you basically needed to use that Esterbrook 356 pen on a particular kind of Strathmore paper?

**CB:** Well, I found that I needed to draw from the white paper up, and draw with a blue pencil that would erase easily, and get my drawing set

**DA:** And you enjoyed the inking, right? You said that the inking was a snap?

**CB:** Yes. I worked out the exact expressions that I wanted, the direct action, everything that went into that drawing—the direction the eyes were looking. . . . Yeah, that was the thing, the charm that made my artwork so popular was that I did the thinking of what the ducks were going to do—I drew it all with the pencil. I inked it all, and it was all my work from just white paper all the way up to the finished product. It gave my stuff a kind of sincerity, the stuff looked genuine.
