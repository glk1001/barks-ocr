# 194 CARL BARKs: CoNVERSatIONS

**CB:** I believe that as time goes by people will realize those paintings I've done are all based on stories. If they don't know the story, the painting will be kind of meaningless, and so I believe the stories will be the thing that lives on into posterity.

**DA:** Do you think that if the stories don't live on, that the paintings won't either?

**CB:** I just don't know. I don't know if they'll—if a couple of generations come along that have never read my stories, those paintings will become just meaningless pictures of funny animals.

**DA:** Do you really think that?

**CB:** I believe so.

**DA:** I didn't expect you to say that actually. (Laughs) Because you've put so much of your heart and soul into these paintings, I mean, it's been a bigger part of your career that anything else. It's been . . .

**CB:** Yes, but that's my workaholic way of living—if I don't paint something that I can feel a little pride in, I don't want to paint it in the first place. I like to outdo my own expectations and outdo whatever the guy expected when he ordered the painting.

**DA:** Which did you like better—did you prefer doing them on a one-to-one basis with individual collectors, or did you like the idea of doing something that would be so good that a whole bunch of people would want to be bidding on it

**CB:** Well, I believe that I've always felt safer when I'm doing a painting that is sort of ordered, one that somebody specified that they would like to have, and that I'm given a lot of leeway on how to do it—that seems to me the ideal method. It takes me so long, and it's such difficult work to paint one of these things, that to paint one and then find that I had painted a fiasco, and it was a flop—it would be kind of hurtful. So I like to feel that somebody is already going to pay for this thing. I'll go ahead and give him his money's worth and more.

**DA:** Gary Arlington was the first one to give you real freedom of subject matter—and you painted a money bin, and those money bins became the most popular . . .

**CB:** Well, I don't remember that that's the way it came about, but anyway, it was a good choice all right. Yeah, I guess it just came to me—all that money, it made your stories so successful—that maybe people would be interested in what it was like in that money bin. Yeah, and it has been—God, you know people just love to just see pictures of that money.

# Donald Ault and Lynda Ault / 1997 195

**DA:** Do you get tired of painting the money?

**CB:** Oh, it becomes quite tiresome. But it is something that I know I can do if I just have the time. I can just sit and paint those coins by the thousands, year after year. (Laughs)

**DA:** I think you told me years ago that it took about four of those money lakes before you figured out how to paint coins more quickly and efficiently.

**CB:** Yeah, that's right, so that they would look like money does—you know, money doesn't just stack up like grains of wheat. They have these flat surfaces. It's like having a whole pile of cookies, and they kind of slide around on top of each other. Yeah, that money bin stuff appeals to people. I would put a lot of fake jewels in there, too. It's strange, that people like those, like Uncle Scrooge in that excessive amount of money.

**DA:** Do you have plans to do any more drawings and paintings?

**CB:** Well, I have an order from some Germans to paint a picture of one of the old comic book covers. I'm not so sure I'm going to be able to do a good job on it, because as I've grown older, my hand is shaky, especially in the forenoons, and my eyesight is getting so bad from macular degeneration that I can't focus on things. So that painting a picture now, I have to get so close to it and have plenty of rest for my hand, and to paint all that stuff with a limp brush—it's a watercolor brush, it's a very fine quality of sable I have, but it is limp. I don't know whether I'm going to be able to do it, but I'll try to get this painting done. That, I believe, will be the last one I will ever do—painting. But these colored pencils, I can do those because you have to bear down so hard on those pencils to get the pigment to come off on the paper that the shaking of my hand is all taken care of because it's just ground into the surface of the paper there where it can't shake.

**DA:** I know those lines were incredibly smooth on those pencil drawings you did for the book. Do you know what cover it is that you've been asked to do that might be your last painting?

**CB:** Oh, it was the one where Uncle Scrooge had a blanket, and he was having a little fire going, and he was sending up smoke signals of dollar signs.
